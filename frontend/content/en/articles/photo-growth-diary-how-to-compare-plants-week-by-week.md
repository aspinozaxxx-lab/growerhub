A photo diary is needed for slow changes that the eye gets used to not noticing. The plant stretches out, the leaves become lighter, the internodes become longer, the crown thins out or, on the contrary, becomes denser gradually. If you look every day, it seems that almost nothing changes. A series of photos week by week shows the dynamics more honestly.

The main rule is repeatability. Shoot the plant from one side, in similar lighting, at the same distance and with a clear reference to the date. Otherwise, you will not be comparing height, but different angles, shadows and camera settings. In GrowerHub the photo should be stored next to watering, fertilizing and microclimate charts.

## How to shoot

Select one main shooting point. For the pot, a general front view and, if necessary, a close-up of the problem leaf will do. For a bed or shelving, a general shot of the zone and individual photos of control plants are useful. If there is a ruler, a mark on the wall or the edge of a pot, leave them in the frame: this makes it easier to judge the size.

Don't shoot only the "beautiful" moments. A photo after transplantation, stress, overwatering or processing is often more useful than a perfect shot. If there is damage, remove it large and add a comment: when did you notice what was done before, whether the watering or light regime changed.

## What to compare

Look beyond the height. Leaf color, crown density, size of new leaves, symmetry of growth, dry edges, spots, and rate of recovery after watering are important. If the plant is stretching, check the light. If new leaves are smaller, look at nutrition, roots and general stress. If the photo shows sluggishness with a wet graph, overflow is possible.

Photos are especially useful when paired with a journal. The entry “changed the light” does not say much in itself. But a series of photos after two weeks shows whether the plant has become more compact. How to keep such records is described in the article [plant diary](/articles/dnevnik-rasteniya-chto-zapisyvat).

## Connection with graphs

Sometimes the graph looks normal, but the photo shows deterioration. This is a signal to look for the missing parameter: light, temperature, ventilation, growth stage, feeding or root condition. The opposite also happens: the graph frightens with a short peak, but photos and inspection show that the plant is not damaged.

In GrowerHub it is convenient to view photos on the same time line where watering, humidity and microclimate are visible. Then the cycle report becomes not a set of individual images, but a care history. There is an article about reading data without panic [history of microclimate and graphics](/articles/istoriya-mikroklimata-i-grafiki).

## Frequency

For most houseplants, one photo per week is sufficient. After a transplant, illness or change in regimen, you can remove it more often, but it is better not to draw conclusions based on one day. For seedlings the changes are faster, so two or three photos a week can be helpful. For a greenhouse, it is convenient to link pictures to a weekly inspection.

## Conclusion

A photo diary works when the pictures are comparable. The same perspective, date, comment and connection to events help to see real changes. This approach makes GrowerHub's advice verifiable: you can see not only what the system recommended, but also how the plant responded.
