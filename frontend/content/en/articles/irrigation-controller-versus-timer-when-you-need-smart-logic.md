The timer and controller solve a similar problem only at first glance. Both can turn on the pump, but make the decision differently. The timer works according to time: for example, every day at 8:00 for two minutes. The controller looks at the state of the zone: soil moisture, pause after last watering, water limit, accidents and maintenance mode. Therefore, the choice does not depend on the word “smart”, but on how predictable the conditions around the plants are.

If you have uniform pots, a stable temperature, and are willing to hand check the soil every few days, a timer may be an okay first step. It is cheaper, simpler and clearer. Its weak point is the lack of feedback. On a hot day, plants may dry out ahead of schedule, after a cool night they may receive excess water, and when the container is empty, the timer will turn on the pump as if everything is in order.

## When the timer runs out

The timer is suitable for short absences, seasonal seedlings or simple drip irrigation with a pre-tested flow rate. In this mode, it is important not to complicate the scheme. Set up short runs, measure the volume of water per run and pause between waterings. If the plants are in a tray, check to see if water is collecting there after two or three cycles in a row.

Even with a timer, it's helpful to keep a simple log. Record the date of setting, duration of operation, approximate volume and condition of the soil after a few hours. Then it will be easier to understand why the plant looks worse: due to the schedule, heat, clogged drip, or an error in dosage. A detailed selection of the system is described in the article [automatic watering for plants](/articles/avtopoliv-dlya-rasteniy-kak-vybrat).

## When you need a controller

A controller is needed where the schedule does not reflect reality. A typical example is a grow box or greenhouse next to a window: the soil dries faster during the day, slower at night, and after ventilation the air humidity changes. The second example is several plants in one zone, where limits are important. The pump should not turn on every ten minutes just because one sensor temporarily shows a dry edge of the pot.

A good controller does not water according to one number. It checks the conditions: whether the sensor was updated long ago or not, whether there was a minimum pause, whether the daily water limit was exceeded, whether there is a leak, whether the service mode is turned on. This logic is especially important if watering works without a person for several days. The principles of safe restrictions are discussed in the article [safe automatic watering](/articles/bezopasnyy-avtopoliv-limity-i-avariynyy-stop).

## The sensor does not replace common sense

A controller is often purchased for the sake of a humidity sensor, but the sensor itself does not make the system reliable. It may be standing too close to the dripper, touching the side of the pot, oxidizing, or showing different values in new and old substrate. Therefore, it is better to introduce watering using sensors gradually: first monitoring, then notifications, then automatic activation with strict limits.

If you want to compare approaches, it is useful to read the material [watering by sensors or by schedule](/articles/poliv-po-datchikam-ili-po-raspisaniyu). The main idea there is simple: the schedule is good as a basic rhythm, the sensor is good as feedback, and the controller is needed to connect them without dangerous jumps.

## What to watch before buying

For a controller, not only the number of channels and a beautiful interface are important. Check if there is a manual start, an event log, a duration limit, blocking in case of an emergency, clear notifications and the ability to continue basic work without the Internet. If a device depends on the cloud for every decision, it is less suitable for watering than local logic with remote viewing.

For the first set, one irrigation channel, a humidity sensor, leakage protection and a normal log are enough. You shouldn’t immediately build a complex scheme for ten conditions. First achieve a repeatable result in one zone, and only then scale it up. A list of basic components is in the article [smart watering starter kit](/articles/startovyy-komplekt-umnogo-poliva).

## Conclusion

The timer is good when the environment is stable and the cost of error is low. A controller is needed when the plants are different, there are more conditions, and watering must take into account the state of the system. For GrowerHub, a practical way is to start with dimensions and history, then add restrictions, then enable automatic rules. So automation does not replace observation, but makes it calmer and more accurate.
